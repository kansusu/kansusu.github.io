I study how people and AI agents build, track, and repair common ground in collaborative dialogue — drawing on Theory of Mind, multimodal perception, and LLM alignment.

Primary Work: Friction-Based Alignment & Theory of Mind

My main line of work asks how dialogue agents should be trained to maintain shared belief states, not just plausible responses. I build on Frictive Policy Optimization (FPO), which treats moments of disagreement, hesitation, and repair not as noise to suppress but as a rational control signal — one that a policy can be trained to read and act on. I extend FPO in two directions: (i) toward an explicit, mechanically computable Theory of Mind, and (ii) toward dialogue where participants hold asymmetric partial information rather than a shared scene.

1. Theory-of-Mind-Grounded Friction

I introduced a four-identifier ToM schema that reconstructs first- and second-order belief states at each referring expression. This makes silent divergence — where both speakers proceed confidently while grounding different referents — mechanically detectable. Ablating the second-order channel collapses misunderstanding detection from 65% to 26%. The corresponding reward-shaping (FAR) and trust-region (FTR) variants of FPO outperform a DPO baseline on task-grounded F1, calibration, and pairwise LLM-judge preference. (Mind the Gap, under review EMNLP 2026.)

2. From Propositional to Perceptual Asymmetry

Existing FPO instantiations assume a shared perceptual context. In real collaborative tasks — HCRC MapTask, OneCommon, our DPIP puzzle — each participant holds a private view, and the same referring expression denotes different entities depending on whose information state grounds it. I generalize the friction functional to a perspective-bound formulation: each participant's $F^{-}$ is evaluated against their own information horizon. A small open-weights model with perspective-bound contradiction detection saturates repair-productive friction recall where GPT-5 under omniscient access does not. (From Propositional to Perceptual Asymmetry, under review SIGDIAL 2026.)

3. Theory of Mind in Multimodal HCI

Earlier in this line, I argued for applying Simulation Theory of Mind to model beliefs and intentions in multi-party collaborative interaction, yielding the epistemic substrate that the later FPO work operationalizes as a trainable signal. (Modeling Theory of Mind in Multimodal HCI, HCII 2024.)

Common Ground Tracking & Epistemic Modeling

A complementary thread of my work asks how shared belief states can be tracked computationally as dialogue unfolds. I designed the Distributed Partial Information Puzzle (DPIP), a multi-party task in which every participant holds a fully disjoint view of a target structure; benchmarked LLM-based and axiomatic dynamic-epistemic-logic paradigms; authored multimodal annotation guidelines for common-ground and friction labels; and contributed the block-description transfer module to the real-time TRACE common-ground demo. The associated Common Ground Tracking system has been the most-cited paper from this line of work. (See Common Ground Tracking section.)

Multimodal Perception & Situational Awareness

To support epistemic modeling, I developed 2D and 3D perception pipelines that extract verbal and non-verbal events — posture, gesture, gaze, object movement, facial expression — from audio-video recordings and live streams. These feed into end-to-end situational-awareness systems for collaborative problem solving, integrating multimodal features via late-fusion classifiers. (See Multimodal Perception section.)

Other Work: Computational Semantics & Cross-Lingual UMR

Earlier in graduate school I worked on Universal Meaning Representation (UMR) annotation for Chinese, identifying language-specific challenges (verb compounds, aspect, anaphora) and proposing compound-annotation solutions adopted in community guidelines. (See Computational Semantics section.)
